I enjoy the act of public discoruse on topics such as history, philosophy, and religion. So naturally I would be drawn to Western Esotericism and it's synthesis of Science, Philosophy, and Religion while being a series of ancient to modern traditions about self actualization within this enchanted worldview that allows itself to experience wonder and awe in a criticalsetting.

The problems with having public discourse about Western Esotericism is that it's foundational knowledge is so vast and personal yet entwined enough with institutional knowlege that the line between intellectual inquiry and unexamined faith gets blurred. So the challenge for a participant in Western Esoteric discourse is to determine what kind of Truth Claim their discourse partners are making so we can determine what kind of discourse is happening. Are we having a historical debate? A theory debate? Or a theological one?

All three of these types of debates need almost explicit ground for the discussion to be meaningful in any capacity. Often it is simple enough to simply listen to or read the words that are being said. A simple "I believe doing X causes Y" is a personal an Esoteric Theory debate. Therefore we are acting as fully enchanted agents and assuming that magick and religion is real without needing to establish proofs that they exist. This is opposed to "Textual evidence suggests practice Y came from X" because this person isn't an active agent in an enchanted world but rather a disenchanted observer trying to figure out how our religious practices came about. 

Both of these discussions are deeply fascinating and I enjoy participating in both. However, I've often seen arguments that try to mix the two types of discussions in an unfounded way. "My practice of Y revealed that X happened and it's a fact" is not the basis of a conducive conversation on the simple grounds it's not useful evidence to anyone but you. There is value in revealed knowledge or gnosis, but without anything tangible to explain that claim, it can only exist in an enchanted theological discussion and should never be brought into a disenchanted historical debate.

Gnosis isn't lesser knowledge it is simply requires more faith and less reason to be used by others, but for yourself it should be used as constructively as possible. Be it inspiration for examining everything about the gnosis you've obtained or using it simply as a motivational idea. And in reverse, disenchanted historical data should never be used to cause distress and harm to others unless it is to argue against harmful to others enchanted world views. 

In short, I am asking for nuance in the world of Esoteric discussion. Knowing when and where our knowledge is wanted and useful makes all the difference in the perceptions of such a strange community. 

## Truth Claim

Truth is an impossible thing to know in the context of human history and in the context of theology. Which you are practicing a theology if you're a witch, a spiritualist, or a ritual magician. It's not a large organized religion, but it is a theological practice. There is only so close we can get to the Truth and the distance between the truth we know and the truth we can communicate is impossibly large as well. Human communication is our single most valuable characteristic, but there's limits to what the written and spoken word can convey. That is why I only consider statements of "facts" as "Truth Claims", things we claim to be true but the real truth of the matter is impossible to convey.

For our purposes, Truth Claims will be our generic term for all evidence based knowledge that we want to present as a basis for a larger discussion. This is represented by it's two sub categories: Historical Data and Gnosis. 

## Historical Data (Keep Working)

>"We must distinguish between history as an event in the past (History-E) and history as what is written about select events in the past (History-W)." -Robert B. Stewart "When did Jesus Become God?: A Christological Debate"

History is interesting because it is the synthesis of things we know about the past and a logical arguement about what those things imply so that we can have a pleasing narative with which to communicate events of the past. This gives us two places to argue within the field. The first is on what we know or the Historical Data. The second being the interpretation of events by the author of a historical narrative. If a specific narrative is backed by a majority of historians, this is called an ["Academic Consensus."](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IOJcpIOH-r4) (1) Something being a consensus doesn't mean it can't be criticized, only that the academic community is generally okay with this explenation and the barrier to convince people otherwise is much higher than on fringe ideas.

There is a real event and a real explanation of events that happened in history. It is just impossible to know it by virtue of us having not been there or not being able to convey the whole event. It's a limitation on time travel and language that prevents historical truth from being known, but we can get a really good idea with enough historical data.

Historical Data is composed of a lot of different things. Primarily it is the two catagories of archeological evidence and textual evidence. These two things help us build a general context such as what life was like in a specific time and place. (Write about the Carolingian Education and how we know that they wrote down their plans for expanding religious education and we have evidence of carolingian era chapels and monasteries being built to educate people)

(Might not Keep) Our focus here is on the Historical Data. The bar for something being historical data is often fairly low. For much of much of Christian Biblical history, we simply need corroberation to verify the basic facts of Jesus having existed, having been a Jewish itinerant preacher, and having been executed by crucifixtion. This is supported only by having several sources all claiming one or all of these facts about someone called Jesus who probably existed between 6 B.C.E and 30 C. E. and they chose to write down this information some time relatively close to the life of Jesus.
